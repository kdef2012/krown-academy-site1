It is not a traditional class — it is a project-based learning experience where students earn, manage, grow, invest, and risk REAL money over the course of a school year, culminating in the launch of a real student-run business.This program is available to 11th and 12th graders who have maintained a 2.0+ GPA. It runs as an elective course worth 1 full credit toward graduation.THE BIG IDEAEvery student starts the year with a $100 deposit into their personal Krown Credit Union account. Over the course of the year, they grow that money through academic performance, financial literacy milestones, and approved fundraising activities. When they reach the investment threshold, they pitch a business plan to the Krown Investment Board. If approved, they receive funding to launch their business — with a safety net that guarantees them a second chance if it fails.WHY THIS MATTERSMost at-risk youth have never had anyone teach them about money, credit, investing, or entrepreneurship. They’ve never seen $500 in an account with their name on it. They’ve never written a business plan. They’ve never experienced the rush of earning money from something they built. This program gives them ALL of that — with real stakes, real money, and real consequences, inside a safety net designed by someone who understands what it’s like to start from nothing.THE KROWN CREDIT UNIONWhat It IsThe Krown Credit Union (KCU) is an in-house school banking system. Every enrolled student has a personal KCU account tracked in a ledger (digital spreadsheet or simple banking app). The KCU operates like a real financial institution — students see deposits, withdrawals, balances, interest, and transaction histories. The ledger is updated weekly and displayed publicly (with student permission) on a classroom leaderboard.Opening DepositEach student receives a $100 opening deposit at the start of the school year. This money is REAL — it comes from the Krown Academy operating budget, grant funding, or community sponsors. The $100 is not a simulation. It sits in a real KVegas Elite Inc bank account earmarked for student accounts. Students track their balance but do not have direct access to the cash until an approved withdrawal (business launch or end-of-year disbursement).Cost to the school: 15 students in the program × $100 = $1,500. This can be funded by: a single local business sponsor, a small grant from Winston-Salem Foundation, a KVegas Elite fundraiser, or allocated from the operating budget.How Money GrowsStudents grow their KCU balance through five channels:1. A student with $200 earns $4/month. A student with $500 earns $10/month. By spring, students with high balances are watching their money grow on its own — and they FEEL it.THE INVESTMENT THRESHOLD & BUSINESS LAUNCHThe ThresholdWhen a student’s KCU balance reaches $500, they unlock the right to pitch a business plan to the Krown Investment Board. They do not have to pitch — they can continue growing their account. But the option becomes available.The $500 threshold is intentional. Starting with $100 and reaching $500 requires effort, discipline, and smart choices. The journey from $100 to $500 IS the financial literacy curriculum in action. Students who reach it have already demonstrated the mindset needed to handle an investment.The Business PlanBefore pitching, students must complete a formal business plan. The Board consists of:Coach Nelson (permanent chair), one teacher/staff member (rotating), one community member or local business owner (invited guest — this creates real-world accountability and networking for students), and one senior student who has already successfully launched a business (Year 2+ of the program).Pitch Day Process:The student presents their business plan in a formal 10-minute pitch followed by 5 minutes of questions from the Board. They must dress professionally. They must present with confidence. This is modeled after Shark Tank — but with a safety net.The Board evaluates on: feasibility (can this actually work?), financial projections (do the numbers make sense?), effort and preparation (did the student do the work?), and passion (does the student believe in this?). The Board votes: Approved, Approved with Conditions (revise and re-pitch), or Not Yet Ready (student continues growing their account and revises their plan).Approval rate should be HIGH. This is not about gatekeeping — it’s about preparation. If a student did the work, their plan should be approved. The Board’s job is to make the plan better, not to reject it.Funding the BusinessOnce approved, the student’s requested investment amount is withdrawn from their KCU account and provided as cash, a prepaid debit card, or direct purchase of materials (Coach Nelson helps with procurement). The student now has real money to execute their plan.THE KROWN SAFETY NETThis is what makes the program transformative.At-risk youth are often paralyzed by the fear of failure. They’ve been told their whole lives that they’ll fail. Many have watched adults in their lives fail financially. The idea of risking money — even money they earned — can trigger deep anxiety.The Krown Safety Net removes the catastrophic downside while preserving the real stakes.How It WorksBefore any investment is disbursed, we record the student’s pre-investment KCU balance.Example: A student has $620 in their account. Their business plan requests $400 in startup capital. Before we release the $400, we record: “Jamal’s pre-investment balance: $620.” His account drops to $220 (the $400 is now invested in his business).If the business succeeds:Jamal keeps all profits. His KCU account grows. He can reinvest, expand, or save. The profit is HIS. At the end of the year, he can withdraw his full balance as real cash.If the business fails:Jamal’s KCU account is restored to his pre-investment balance of $620. He loses nothing. He is given a mandatory “Failure Debrief” session (see below) and then given a SECOND opportunity to pitch a new or revised business plan.The Safety Net activates ONE time per student per year.If the second business also fails, the student keeps whatever is left in their account but does not receive a third restoration. This teaches that safety nets have limits — in life, you get chances, but not infinite chances.The Failure DebriefWhen a business fails, the student does NOT experience shame. They experience a structured learning moment.Coach Nelson meets with the student 1-on-1. The debrief covers: What happened? (facts, not feelings first). What did you learn? What would you do differently? Was the failure in the plan, the execution, or the market? What skills did you develop even though the business didn’t succeed? The student writes a 1-page “Failure Report” that is kept in their portfolio. This report is framed as a badge of honor — every successful entrepreneur has failures. The student is then encouraged (not required) to develop a new plan.The message: Failure is not the end. Failure is data. Kings and Queens learn from failure and come back stronger. That’s what earning your Krown means.FINANCIAL LITERACY CURRICULUM (9 UNITS)The financial literacy content is taught during Academic Block 3 once per week (Fridays, replacing one period of Science/History). REAL MONEY.This is not a simulation. Not play money. Not a spreadsheet exercise. Students hold real dollars, make real decisions, and experience real consequences. When a student sees their balance go from $100 to $623, that’s not a grade — it’s proof that they can build something.2. THE SAFETY NET.No other program gives students real investment money AND a guaranteed second chance. This is the intersection of Coach Nelson’s Psychology background and his entrepreneurship vision. At-risk youth need to learn that failure is not fatal — and the only way to learn that is to fail safely.3. THE COMPOUNDING IDENTITY SHIFT.A student who starts the year thinking “I’m not good with money” and ends the year holding $800 they earned through grades, discipline, and a business they built — that student’s identity has fundamentally changed. They are no longer “at-risk.” They are an entrepreneur. They are a King. They are a Queen. They have earned their Krown.4. MEDIA AND GRANT APPEAL.A school where at-risk youth run real businesses funded by an in-house credit union with a safety net designed by a former at-risk youth with Sociology and Psychology degrees? That’s a front-page story. That’s a grant application that funds itself. That’s the program that puts Krown Academy on the map.5. ALUMNI NETWORK.Students who graduate from the Krown Credit Union program leave with: a real bank account (opened in Unit 2), business experience, a portfolio, a professional pitch on video, and connections to community business leaders who served on their Investment Board. This is a pipeline to employment, entrepreneurship, and college business programs.Build Your Kingdom. Earn Your Krown.
